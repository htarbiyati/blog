Lagrange Interpolation

Posted Nov 3, 2020 2020-11-03T07:41:03+00:00 by Homayoon

Theoretical Foundation: The Interpolation Formula of Lagrange

In what follows, we denote by \(\Pi_n\) the set of all real or complex polynomials \(P\) whose degress do not exceed \(n\):

\[P(x)=a_0+a_1x+\dots+a_nx^n\]

Theorem 2.1.1.1 1

For \(n+1\) arbitrary support points
\((x_i,f_i),\hspace{8pt} i=0,\dots,n,\hspace{8pt} x_i \neq x_k\) for \(i \neq k\)
there exists a unique polynomial \(P \in \Pi_n\) with \(P(x_i)=f_i, \hspace{8pt} i=0,1,\dots,n\).

PROOF. Uniqueness: For any tow polynomials \(P_1, P_2 \in \Pi_n\) with

\[P_1(x_i)=P_2(x_i)=f_i, \hspace{8pt} i=0,1,\dots,n,\]

the polynomial \(P:=P_1-P_2 \in \Pi_n\) has degree at most \(n\), and it has at least \(n+1\) different zeroes, namely \(x_i,i=0,\dots,n\).\(P\) must therefore vanish identically, and \(P_1=P_2\).

Existence: We will construct the interpolating polynomial \(P\) explicitly with the help of polynomials \(L_i \in \Pi_n,\ i=0,\dots,n,\) for which

\[L_i(x_k)=\delta_{ik}= \begin{cases} 1 & i=k \\ 0 & i \neq k \end{cases}\]

The following Lagrange polynomials satisfy the above conditions:

\(L_i(x):\equiv \frac{(x-x_0)\dots(x-x_{i-1})(x-x_{i+1})\dots(x-x_{n})}{(x_i-x_0)\dots(x_{i}-x_{i-1})(x_i-x_{i+1})\dots(x_{i}-x_{n})} \equiv \frac{\omega (x)}{(x-x_{i})\omega'(x_i)}\) with \(\omega (x):=\prod_{i=0}^{n}(x-x_i)\) Our proof so far shows that the Lagrange polynomials are uniquely determined by \(L_i(x_k)\).

The solution \(P\) of the interpolation problem can now be expressed directly in terms of the polynomials \(L_i\), leading to the Lagrange interpolation formula: \(P(x) \equiv \sum_{i=0}^{n} f_i L_i(x)=\sum_{i=0}^{n} f_i \prod_{\substack{k \neq i \\ k=0}}^{n} \frac{x-x_k}{x_i-x_k}\)

for i=1:N+1
    x(i)=input('Insert your next X data= ');
    y(i)=input('insert your next y data= ');
end
xstar=input('Enter the value you want to interploate: ');
disp(' ')

Pn = 0;

for j = 1:N+1
    L(j) = 1; 
    for i = 1:N+1
        if i~=j
            L(j) = L(j)*(xstar-x(i)) / (x(j)-x(i));
        end
    end
    
    L(j) = L(j)*y(j);
    Pn = Pn+L(j);
end

disp('The interpolated y-value of xstar is equal to: ');

% https://uk.mathworks.com/help/matlab/ref/vander.html
% An alternate form of the Vandermonde matrix flips the matrix along the vertical axis, as shown.
% Use fliplr(vander(v)) to return this form.
% V => Vandermonde Matrix
V = fliplr(vander(x));
A = inv(V)*y';

% https://uk.mathworks.com/help/symbolic/poly2sym.html
% Have to reverse matrix to get proper form for representation
Pn = poly2sym(flip(A))
